Approach Valuation Methodologies

Qualitative Approach

•Berkus Method

•The Berkus Method assesses the value of a startup based on qualitative factors critical to the business's early development and future success. These factors typically include the strength of the idea, prototype, management team, strategic relationships, and the ability to roll out the product or service. The process is designed to assign a pre-money valuation by determining the degree of achievement for each factor.

•Key Milestones:

— Sound Idea The strength of the business idea, the core concept that drives the startup.
— Prototype/Product Development The progress made in developing a working prototype or proof of concept.
— Quality Management Team The capability and experience of the management team to execute the business plan.
— Strategic Relationships Partnership with customers, suppliers, distributors, or intellectual property, relationships that increase the company’s chances of success.
— Product Rollout/Sales The stage at which the product is ready for market or has already been launched, indicating traction.

•Valuation Process:

1.Assign maximum value to each milestone, ensuring the total pre-money valuation does not exceed $2.5 million when all milestones are fully achieved.

2.Determine the achievement percentage for each milestone based on the startup’s progress, ranging from 0% to 100%.

3.Multiply the maximum value of each milestone by its respective achievement percentage to calculate its contribution to the total pre-money valuation.

4.Sum the contributions of all milestones to arrive at the total pre-money valuation.

5.Add the investment amount to the pre-money valuation to calculate the post-money valuation.

6.Divide the investment amount by the post-money valuation and multiply by 100 to determine the investor's ownership percentage.

Approach Valuation Methodologies

Qualitative Approach

•Risk Factor Summation Method.

•The Risk Factor Summation (RFS) method is one of several valuation methodologies used to determine the value of startups at the seed stage, particularly when financial projections are either unavailable or unreliable. The RFS method uses a set of risk factors to adjust the pre-money valuation based on qualitative assessments.

•The Risk Factor Summation (RFS) method assesses a startup's risk profile across 12 factors, such as management team quality, product development progress, and market risk, each scored from +2 to −2. Positive scores indicate lower risk, while negative scores reflect higher risk.

•The total score from the 12 risk factors is applied to adjust the startup's benchmark pre-money valuation, either increasing or decreasing it based on the overall risk profile.

Qualitative Approach

•Scorecard Method

•Scorecard method assign weights to seven key factors (e.g., Team, Opportunity, Product), ensuring the total is 100%, and adjust weights based on the importance of each factor.

•Rate the target company against a benchmark (average company) for each factor as a percentage. Scores greater than 100% indicate outperformance, less than 100% indicate underperformance, and 100% means equal to the benchmark.

•Multiply each factor's weight by its relative score (percentage) and sum the results. Apply this adjustment to the median pre-money valuation to estimate the company's valuation.

Market-Based Approach

•Venture Capital Method

The Venture Capital (VC) method estimates the value of a startup based on its potential future exit value while accounting for investment risks and expected returns. Here's the process:

•Estimate Future Exit Value: Calculate the projected value of the company at exit (e.g., acquisition or IPO) using revenue or earnings multiples.

•Adjust for Probability of Failure: Multiply the future exit value by the probability of success to account for startup risks.

•Calculate Required Ownership Stake: Determine the percentage ownership investors need to achieve their required annual return over the investment horizon.

•Calculate Post-money Valuation: Use the required ownership stake to back-calculate the company’s post-money valuation.

•Incorporate Dilution Effects: Adjust ownership stake and valuation to account for dilution from future funding rounds and employee stock option grants.
